V. parahaemolyticus

Vibrio parahaemolyticus, an emerging zoonotic pathogen, is able to cause rapid cell death using a type III secretion system on it's first chromosome. Although the links to pathogenicity are not completely clear, what is clear is that this molecular weapon is a powerful tool against human cells. Focusing on how T3SS-1 is acivated, we aim to outline how T3SS-1 performs in Vibrio parahaemolyticus borne gastroenteritis.

Genetic Activation of the T3SS

V. parahaemolyticus is an ocean microbe that is found worldwide, albeit more commonly in warmer ocean waters. These microbes, like most Vibrios have complex interactions with a variety of ocean plants and animals, although most importantly V. parahaemolyticus can colonize oysters. This colonization is the main entry of V. parahaemolyticus into the human food chain, where certain strains are able to cause an gastrointestinal infection that leads to an acute, generally self-limiting diarrhea. However, this infection can progress to be more serious or life-threatening in the extremes of age (very young/very old) and the immunocomprimised.

Vibrio parahaemolyticus has two T3SS's (T3SS-1 and T3SS-2) which it utilizes for host cell cytotoxicity and enterotoxicity respectively. Our lab is primarily focused on T3SS-1, which is implicated in rapid host cell cytoxicity in vitro, and clinical isolates containing only the T3SS-1 have been isolated.

Our lab has been investigating the master regulator for T3SS-1, exsA. This regulator is a member of the AraC family of transcriptional regulators, and is itself regulated by a series of complex mechanisms at both the protein and genetic level. Two promoters generate the exsA transcript: the exsB-exsA cotranscript and the exsA trasncript, generated by the exsB and exsA promters respectively. However, a large intergeneic region upstream of exsA, which contains the exsA promoter, regulates much of the exsA transcript found in the cell. By studying this exsA promoter region, we hope to elucidate the mechanism's of exsA genetic activation, so that we can understand the activation of the T3SS-1 foundationally.

Recent research from our lab has identified HlyU, a regulator of a hemolysin in Vibrio cholerae, as a genetic activator of exsA. Using a transposon mutant library, in a unique reporter strain containing the lux reporter attached to the the exsA promtoer region, integrated into the chromosome, we not only identified HlyU as a genetic activaotr, but also verified other studies that identified H-NS as a major repressor of the exsA locus.

To follow up on this research, our lab is currently looking for other regulators and interrogating the binding site for HlyU in the exsA promoter.

Chitin and N-acetylglucosamine utilization and its links to pathogenesis

Many human pathogens are able to use N-acetylglucosamine, a 5-carbon sugar, as a carbon source. Further, many are able to detect N-acetylglucosamine and these sensing mechanisms are required for adequate virulence. For example, it has been shown that N-acetylglucosamine sensing in C. albicans is necessary for human virulence.

Vibrio spp. are ancestrally environmental organisms that have become human pathogens. More importantly, Chitin, an N-acetylglucoasmine polymer found as the second most abundant natural carbon polymer in the world, is a primarly food source for these bacteria in the ocean. Recent studies have shown that V. cholerae's ability to bind Chitin and N-acetylglucosamine, through the N-acetylglucosamine binding protein A (GbpA), is necessary for human colonization and infection.

Our lab has recently begun exploring how N-acetylglucosamine, and chitin, are utilized by Vibrio parahaemolyticus and what connections this utilization might have to human colonization and virulence. We are investigating an innovative technique called transposon sequencing (Tn-Seq) to do this. Using population genomics, tied to transposon mutagenesis, this technique allows us to identify genome-wide mutations that are important for a particular process.
